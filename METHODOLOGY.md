# The Triple Method: A Human-Centered Learning System

Traditional mathematics education often relies on sequential memorization (learning tables from 1 to 12 in order) and passive studying. For neurodivergent learners, particularly those with ADHD, this approach can lead to frustration, cognitive overload, and a severe drop in self-confidence.

The **Triple Method** was developed to bypass the friction of mental calculation by establishing a rock-solid foundation of pure memorization. By combining physical engagement, structured breaks, and digital gamification, this system transforms a stressful chore into an interactive challenge.

---

## 🧠 Core Occupational Therapy (OT) Principles

Guided by Occupational Therapy practices, this methodology is built around three strict rules designed for ADHD-friendly learning:

### 1. The 20/15 Time Limit Rule
Attention spans in children with ADHD drop exponentially after extended periods of focus.
* **Learning sessions must never exceed 20 minutes.**
* Every 20-minute work block must be followed by a **mandatory 15-minute physical break**. Pushing through frustration does not build memory; it builds resistance.

### 2. Occupied Hands (Fidgeting)
Learners with ADHD often absorb information better when their hands are actively engaged, which channels excess energy. During written exercises, the learner must hold a sensory fidget tool (we successfully used a *NeeDoh Fuzz Ball*) in their non-dominant hand.

### 3. Progressive Confidence Building
We do not learn the tables in sequential order (1, 2, 3...). Instead, we group them by logical difficulty. This ensures the learner experiences early, frequent victories, building the momentum needed for harder numbers.

* **Seed Tables (1, 2, 5, 10):** The easiest patterns. They establish the logical structure of multiplication.
* **Construction Tables (3, 4, 6, 11):** The bridge. The learner understands the game and begins flexing their memory muscles.
* **Advanced Tables (7, 8, 9, 12):** The final challenge. By this point, the logic is fully understood, and the task is pure memorization.

*Recommended Pace:* 2 tables per day. Expect the entire process to take between 6 to 12 days, depending on the child's energy levels and daily constraints.

## 🛠️ Step-by-Step Execution: The Triple Method

For every table you study, you must execute these three steps sequentially.

### Method 1: The Grid (Visual, Auditory, and Mechanical)
**Goal:** Map the logic and encode visual memory.
**Tools needed:** Printed 12x12 Grid PDF, a pencil, and a fidget tool.

1. **Download the Grids:** [English printable grid](assets/printables/matpro-grid-en.pdf) · [Spanish printable grid](assets/printables/matpro-grid-es.pdf)
2. **Setup:** The learner holds the pencil in their dominant hand and the fidget tool in the other.
3. **Execution:** The learner fills out the grid for the specific table they are learning (e.g., Table of 8, from 8x1 to 8x12). 
4. **The Golden Rule:** They *must* speak out loud while doing it. They read the coordinate, say the answer, and write it simultaneously (e.g., speaking *"Eight times three is twenty-four"* while writing `24`).
5. **Consistency:** Let the learner choose how they read the grid (Row x Column, or Column x Row), but once they pick a format, force them to stick to it to build a predictable cognitive structure.

### Method 2: The Object Toss (Kinesthetic and Active Recall)
**Goal:** Transition from visual reliance to pure memory under mild, fun pressure.
**Tools needed:** A soft, tossable object (like the NeeDoh Fuzz Ball).

1. **Execution:** The tutor and learner stand apart. The tutor throws the ball and calls out an operation (e.g., "Six times four!").
2. **Response:** The learner catches the ball, recalls the answer strictly from memory (no finger counting allowed!), says the answer, and throws the ball back.
3. **The Progression:**
   * *First pass:* Sequential order (x1 going up to x12).
   * *Second pass:* Reverse order (x12 going down to x1).
   * *Third pass:* Completely random.
4. **The "Step-Back" Rule for Mistakes:** If the learner gives a wrong answer, **do not tell them the correct answer.** Instead, step back to the previous question they got right. If they miss `6x4`, ask them `6x3` again, wait for them to get `18`, and then immediately ask `6x4` again. This forces their brain to rebuild the logical bridge themselves.

### Method 3: Matpro App (Interactive Digital Reinforcement)
**Goal:** Gamify the repetition process, track progress, and cement the knowledge.
**Tools needed:** A computer or tablet running the Matpro Web App.

1. **Setup:** Open the Matpro web application.
2. **Practice Tables Mode:** Select only the table(s) practiced that day. Choose difficulty, number of questions, and time per question. Setting the timer to `0` creates an untimed round.
3. **Maxitables Pro Mode:** Use this only when the learner is ready for endurance practice. It covers every multiplication from 1x1 through 12x12 for a full 144-question round.
4. **Smart Distractors:** The app uses logical wrong answers, such as nearby table values or reversed digits, to test real memory instead of guesswork.
5. **Feedback:** At the end of the round, sit together and review the "Error Review" screen to see exactly which numbers require more focus during the next session.

---
*By consistently applying these three steps, observing the time limits, and keeping the environment positive and gamified, foundational mathematics can shift from a source of anxiety to a source of pride.*
